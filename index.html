About Charles Spurgeon

Charles Spurgeon was an English Particular Baptist preacher. Spurgeon remains highly influential among Christians of various denominations, to some of whom he is known as the "Prince of Preachers." He was a strong figure in the Baptist tradition, defending the 1689 London Baptist Confession of Faith, and opposing the liberal and pragmatic theological tendencies in the Church of his day.

The Blood of the Lamb

Scripture: John 1:29

This sermon teaches that the victory of believers over Satan and sin comes through the blood of the Lamb—Jesus Christ’s sacrificial death on the cross. Satan is portrayed as a powerful enemy who accuses and deceives humanity, but Christ has already defeated him through His death and resurrection.

The blood of Christ represents His substitutionary sacrifice, where He took the punishment for humanity’s sins so that believers could be forgiven and justified before God. Because of this sacrifice, Christians have confidence to approach God in prayer and strength to resist temptation.

Believers overcome the enemy by trusting in Christ’s atonement, proclaiming the message of the cross, and living faithfully for God even in the face of hardship. The sermon reminds Christians that the cross is the greatest weapon against sin, despair, and evil, and calls all people to place their faith in Jesus for salvation.

The Blood of the Lamb

Scripture: Luke 14:23

This sermon highlights the urgent call of the Gospel for sinners to come to Christ. Though humanity is lost in sin and unable to save itself, God in His mercy sent Jesus to die for sinners so that whoever believes in Him may receive forgiveness and eternal life.

The message calls those who feel spiritually poor, broken, or lost to respond to God’s invitation today. It warns against delaying repentance and reminds listeners that life is uncertain and judgment is real. While ministers can preach and plead, it is the Holy Spirit who changes hearts and leads people to salvation.

The Blood of the Lamb

Text: Numbers 32:23

This sermon reflects on the tragic mining disaster in Risca, South Wales, where many miners suddenly lost their lives in an explosion. Using this heartbreaking event, the message highlights the deep sorrow of sudden loss and the reality that life can change in a moment. Families who expected their loved ones to return home were instead faced with grief, reminding everyone of the fragile nature of life.

The sermon teaches that sudden bereavement and sudden death should lead people to reflect seriously on their relationship with God. Since life is uncertain and death can come without warning, people should not delay repentance or ignore the call of the Gospel. Believers are also encouraged to cherish their loved ones, pray for their families, and live faithfully while they still have time.

Another key theme is the eternal consequences of death. For those who trust in Christ, death brings a glorious and immediate entrance into eternal joy with God. However, for those who reject Christ, death brings the terrifying reality of judgment and separation from God.

Ultimately, the sermon calls listeners to prepare their hearts today. Life is short and uncertain, but salvation is offered freely through Jesus Christ. Therefore, every person is urged to turn to Christ, trust in His grace, and live with eternity in view.

Life Timeline

1834

Birth of Charles Spurgeon

Born in Kelvedon, Essex, England.

Charles Haddon Spurgeon was born on June 19, 1834. His father and grandfather were both pastors, which strongly influenced his early spiritual formation.

1850

Conversion Experience

Converted during a snowstorm in a Methodist chapel.

At age 15, Spurgeon entered a Primitive Methodist chapel during a snowstorm. A lay preacher spoke on Isaiah 45:22 which led to his conversion.

1854

Pastor in London

Became pastor of New Park Street Chapel.

At just 19 years old, Spurgeon became pastor of New Park Street Chapel in London, which soon became too small for the thousands attending his sermons.

1861

Metropolitan Tabernacle Opens

One of the largest churches in Victorian London.

The Metropolitan Tabernacle opened with seating for over 5,000 people. It became the center of Spurgeon’s preaching ministry.

1892

Death

Passed away in Menton, France.

Spurgeon died on January 31, 1892. His funeral in London drew tens of thousands and his sermons continue to influence readers worldwide.
